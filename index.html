The Thesis

AI is rapidly automating routine thinking. What it does not automate — and what remains scarce — is judgment: the ability to reason clearly when a problem is genuinely new, when there is no clean precedent, when no model has been trained on anything quite like it. It has been the through-line of my career — in the mathematical problems I research, and in the genuinely novel questions my consulting clients bring me.

II.Teaching

Training the kind of thinking AI cannot do.

AI is already absorbing the kind of work that grades and textbooks were designed to reward. The students I teach today will spend their careers solving problems no textbook — and no model — has seen before. What will set them apart is the harder capability: recognizing a genuinely novel problem, breaking it down, and reasoning it through from first principles when no recipe exists.

At Columbia, I teach graduate courses in Statistical Inference, Time Series, and Modeling across the Financial Mathematics, Statistics, and Data Science programs. At Oxford, I tutored undergraduates in mathematics and statistics across a broad range of courses — analysis, optimization, probability, actuarial science — at several of the University's most historic colleges, including Christ Church, Brasenose, Exeter, and Lady Margaret Hall. I also interviewed incoming mathematics undergraduates for Christ Church.

My goal is to leave a student able to stand on their own. That means pushing hard on reasoning from first principles, demanding they defend their thinking to a skeptical expert without notes, and refusing to let them survive on pattern-matching. It also means creating the conditions where the deepest learning happens — an environment where a student feels secure enough to expose the gap in their understanding, rather than hide it. Holding those two together, in my experience, is the whole craft of teaching.

III.Research

Rigorous mathematics, pushed to the AI frontier.

My academic research lies in applied probability — the study of random structures that evolve in time. My central line of work is on tree-valued stochastic processes, the subject of several papers in the leading journals of the field, including the Annals of Probability and Bernoulli. A parallel strand concerns large deviations — the mathematics of rare, high-consequence events, with direct applications in risk management and financial mathematics.

I have always been drawn to applying rigorous mathematics where it can actually change outcomes. Early in my career that meant pioneering logistic-regression credit modeling in Jordanian microfinance — one of the first such applications in the developing world. Today it means working on AI itself: helping organizations make sound decisions about what current models can and cannot do, and contributing to the next generation of systems that can genuinely reason about mathematical problems they have never seen before.

An Illustration

The Brownian continuum random tree — a family of random objects I have spent much of my research on.

The Brownian continuum random tree is one of the most important objects in modern probability theory: a universal scaling limit that emerges whenever you zoom out far enough from any of a broad class of random branching structures. It appears in the genealogies of populations evolving under branching, in the critical regime of large random graphs, in the geometry of random surfaces (as the tree underlying the Brownian map), and in the scaling limits of branching random walks and epidemic models. The tree on the right is sampled live using Aldous's line-breaking construction; each new sample is a fresh independent draw.

My 2018 paper with M. Winkel in the Annals of Probability, and later work with N. Chee and M. Winkel in Bernoulli, developed new recursive methods for constructing these objects. See publications below →

Private Mentoring

Mentoring for the very top.

Each year, I take on a small group of exceptional young people — usually by introduction, usually with real ambition to reach the top of their field. The goal isn't another tutor and isn't the next exam result. It's to help them become the kind of person who can think well in a world where AI will do most of the easy thinking for them.

Every engagement is tailored. I begin with a careful reading of the student — their ambitions, their strengths, the specific gaps holding them back, and the longer horizon they are building toward. The work is designed around them, and it evolves as they do.

For some, the focus is advanced mathematics, statistics, or preparation for graduate research. For others, it is academic positioning and decisions about where — and how — to aim. For nearly all of them, a meaningful part of the work is understanding AI itself: how it actually operates under the hood, where it will genuinely reshape the professions they are entering, and where human judgment will remain decisive.

Each engagement is shaped around the student and the goals the family brings — from a focused semester on a specific preparation to a long-horizon mentorship measured in years. Whatever the timeframe, the aim is the same: to leave the student visibly more capable of thinking for themselves, and better prepared for whatever they are aiming at next.

  • Advanced mathematics, statistics, and quantitative reasoning Developing the kind of mathematical maturity required for the most demanding university programs and research careers.
  • Understanding AI — properly How today's models actually work, what they cannot yet do, and what that means for the next twenty years of work, investing, and leadership.
  • Long-horizon judgment Choices about education, career, and life direction that will still look right a decade from now — not only this admissions cycle.

Engagements are selective and typically by introduction. References and practical arrangements are shared directly, on mutual interest.
